## üéØ **Understanding Character Arcs**

<details>
<summary><strong>Three Main Arc Types</strong></summary>

### **Positive Change Arc**
Character overcomes their flaw and becomes better
- *Example: Ebenezer Scrooge (A Christmas Carol)*

### **Negative Change Arc**
Character succumbs to their flaw and becomes worse
- *Example: Walter White (Breaking Bad)*

### **Flat Arc**
Character stays consistent while changing the world around them
- *Example: Captain America, Atticus Finch*

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>How to Use This Worksheet</strong></summary>

### **Step-by-Step Process:**
1. **Define the starting point** ‚Äî Who is your character at the beginning?
2. **Identify the lie/truth** ‚Äî What false belief holds them back?
3. **Map key moments** ‚Äî What events force change?
4. **Plan the transformation** ‚Äî How do they end up different?
5. **Ensure causality** ‚Äî Each stage should flow logically from the last

### **Tips:**
- ‚úÖ Make change gradual, not sudden
- ‚úÖ Include setbacks and resistance
- ‚úÖ Connect external plot to internal growth
- ‚úÖ Show evidence of change through actions
- ‚ùå Avoid arbitrary transformations
- ‚ùå Don't skip the struggle

</details>

## ‚öñÔ∏è **Balance Check**

### **Ensure Your Arc Works**

**Internal vs. External:**
- [ ] External plot creates pressure for internal change
- [ ] Internal growth affects ability to solve external problem
- [ ] The two storylines intersect at climax

**Pacing:**
- [ ] Change is gradual, not sudden
- [ ] Character resists before accepting
- [ ] Setbacks feel earned
- [ ] Final transformation is believable

**Causality:**
- [ ] Each stage leads logically to the next
- [ ] Challenges specifically target the character's flaw
- [ ] Growth is earned through struggle
