**Third person limited** (also called "close third" or "third person subjective") uses "he/she/they" pronouns while staying inside one character's perspective at a time. The reader sees the world through that character's eyes and knows their thoughts, but maintains slight narrative distance.

## âœ¨ **Strengths of Third Person Limited**

### **1. Balance of Intimacy and Distance**

Third limited lets readers connect deeply with characters while maintaining narrative breathing room.

**Example:**
> Sarah pressed her palm against the door. Inside, she heard footsteps. Her hand trembledâ€”not from cold, but from certainty. Everything would change the moment she knocked.

**Why it works:** Close to character's experience but with slight narrative perspective.

---

### **2. Flexibility with Multiple Characters**

Can shift POV between chapters or sections to show different perspectives.

**Example:**
> **Chapter 1 - Marcus**
> Marcus watched her leave. He wanted to call after her, to explain. But the words stuck in his throat like glass.
>
> **Chapter 2 - Elena**
> Elena didn't look back. If she did, she'd see him standing there, and she might weaken. She couldn't afford to weaken.

**Why it works:** Each character gets their moment as protagonist.

---

### **3. Easier to Write Than First Person**

"He thought" vs. "I thought"â€”less demanding than maintaining first person voice.

**Example (easier in third):**
> He considered his options. Lying would be easier. But Sarah would know. She always knew.

**Same in first (requires stronger voice):**
> I considered my options. Lying would be easier. But Sarah would know. She always knew.

---

### **4. Reader Sympathy Without Full Investment**

Readers can understand and care without fully *being* the character.

**Example:**
> Tom was wrong. He knew it even as he said it, but pride wouldn't let him back down. He watched her face close off, shuttering like a house in a storm, and hated himself for it.

**Effect:** Readers see Tom's flaws clearly while still sympathizing.

## âš¡ **Types of Third Person Limited**

### **Third Limited Close**

Nearly as intimate as first person, deep in character's head.

**Example:**
> The phone rang. Sarah's heart jumped. Marcus. It had to be Marcus. She reached for it, hand shaking. Don't be desperate, she told herself. Play it cool. But her finger hit answer before she could think.

**Characteristics:**
- Deep access to thoughts
- Character's vocabulary and voice color the narration
- Minimal narrative distance
- Borders on [Deep POV](deep-pov.md)

---

### **Third Limited Distant**

More camera-like, focuses on external observations with some internal access.

**Example:**
> The phone rang. Sarah picked it up on the second ring, her expression carefully neutral. She listened, said nothing for a long moment, then set the phone down without a word.

**Characteristics:**
- More external observation
- Less direct thought access
- Slight narrator presence
- Approaches third omniscient

## ğŸ¯ **Handling Thoughts in Third Limited**

### **Method 1: Direct Thought (Most Common)**

**Present thoughts without tags or italics.**

**Example:**
> She stared at the email. He was lying. Had to be. No one resigned from a job like that without a reason.

**Best for:** Seamless integration, modern style

---

### **Method 2: Italicized Direct Thought**

**Like dialogue, but internal.**

**Example:**
> She stared at the email. *He's lying,* she thought. *Has to be. No one resigns from a job like that without a reason.*

**Best for:** Emphasizing internal voice, distinguishing from narrative

---

### **Method 3: Indirect Thought**

**Filter through narrative voice.**

**Example:**
> She stared at the email. He was lying, she thought. Had to be. No one resigned from a job like that without a reason.

**Best for:** Maintaining distance, older literary style

---

### **Method 4: Free Indirect Discourse**

**Thought and narrative blur together.**

**Example:**
> She stared at the email. Lying. Obviously. Who resigned from a job like that without a reason? Please.

**Best for:** Sophisticated literary fiction, character voice infusing narration

## âš ï¸ **Common Mistakes**

### **1. Head-Hopping**

**Problem:** Switching POV within a scene without clear transition.

**Wrong:**
> Sarah slammed the door. She was furious. Marcus flinched. He knew he'd screwed up. Sarah didn't care about his excuses anymore. Marcus wished he could explain.

**Why it's wrong:** Jumps between Sarah's and Marcus's perspectives mid-scene.

**Right:**
> Sarah slammed the door, fury radiating from her rigid shoulders. Marcus flinched. She didn't look back, didn't give him a chance to explain. Not that he deserved one.

**Fix:** Stay in one head. Show other character through observation only.

---

### **2. Inconsistent Depth**

**Problem:** Varying how deeply you're in the character's head without reason.

**Inconsistent:**
> She entered the room cautiously. The wallpaper was striped. Terror gripped her heart. The carpet was beige.

**Why it's wrong:** Mixing deep emotion with detached observation randomly.

**Consistent:**
> She entered the room cautiously, every nerve alert. Striped wallpaper closed in like prison bars. Beige carpet muffled her footstepsâ€”no warning when someone approached.

**Fix:** Filter everything through character's emotional/mental state.

---

### **3. Thought Tags Everywhere**

**Problem:** "He thought," "she wondered," "he realized" becomes repetitive.

**Weak:**
> He thought about what she'd said. He wondered if she was right. He realized he'd been wrong.

**Strong:**
> What she'd said circled his mind like a vulture. Was she right? God. He'd been wrong all along.

**Fix:** Establish POV clearly, then drop tags except for clarity.

---

### **4. Filtering**

**Problem:** Unnecessary distance words.

**Weak:**
> She saw him enter. She heard the door close. She felt her heart race.

**Strong:**
> He entered. The door closed. Her heart raced.

**Fix:** We're in her POVâ€”we already know she's seeing, hearing, feeling.
