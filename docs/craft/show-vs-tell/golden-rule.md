## ğŸ” **How to Show Instead of Tell**

### **1. Use Sensory Details**

Instead of naming an emotion or state, describe what can be **seen, heard, felt, tasted, or smelled**.

<details>
<summary><b>Examples: Sensory Showing</b></summary>

#### **TELL:**
> The bakery smelled good.

#### **SHOW:**
> Warm butter and cinnamon rolled through the doorway, wrapping around her like a blanket from childhood.

---

#### **TELL:**
> It was a beautiful morning.

#### **SHOW:**
> Sunlight streamed through the leaves, dappling the sidewalk in gold. Birds competed in song from the oak tree, and the air tasted of rain-washed earth.

---

#### **TELL:**
> The room was messy.

#### **SHOW:**
> Clothes draped over the chair, the desk, the lampshade. Coffee rings overlapped on the nightstand. Something under the bed might have been alive once.

</details>

---

### **2. Show Through Action**

Instead of stating facts, show characters **doing things** that reveal the information.

<details>
<summary><b>Examples: Action-Based Showing</b></summary>

#### **TELL:**
> Marcus was nervous about the interview.

#### **SHOW:**
> Marcus tugged his tie straight for the third time in as many minutes. His resume crinkled in his sweating palm.

---

#### **TELL:**
> She was a careful driver.

#### **SHOW:**
> She checked her mirrors twice, signaled early, and kept both hands at ten and two even on the empty country road.

---

#### **TELL:**
> He was exhausted.

#### **SHOW:**
> He fumbled his keys twice before getting them in the lock. Coffee could wait till tomorrow. Hell, brushing his teeth could wait. He collapsed onto the couch still wearing his shoes.

</details>

---

### **3. Use Body Language**

Physical reactions reveal emotion more powerfully than naming feelings.

<details>
<summary><b>Examples: Body Language</b></summary>

#### **TELL:**
> Janet was disappointed.

#### **SHOW:**
> Janet's smile didn't reach her eyes. "That's great," she said, and turned to the window.

---

#### **TELL:**
> Tom was attracted to her.

#### **SHOW:**
> Tom found himself standing straighter when she entered the room, and he'd checked his reflection three times that morning.

---

#### **TELL:**
> She was scared.

#### **SHOW:**
> Her pulse hammered in her throat. The shadows seemed darker than they should be, and every creak of the old house pulled her attention.

</details>

---

### **4. Show Through Dialogue & Subtext**

What characters **say** and **don't say** reveals more than narrative telling.

<details>
<summary><b>Examples: Dialogue Showing</b></summary>

#### **TELL:**
> Their marriage was falling apart.

#### **SHOW:**
> "I made dinner," she said.
>
> He grunted, eyes on his phone.
>

>

#### **TELL:**
> He was lying.

#### **SHOW:**
> "I was at the office," he said, meeting her eyes for a split second before studying his shoes. "You know, the usual. Spreadsheets."
>
> She'd never known him to describe anything as "the usual."

</details>

### **5. Use Specific Details**

Specific, concrete details create pictures; vague generalities tell.

<details>
<summary><b>Examples: Specific vs. Vague</b></summary>

|-----------------|-------------------|

| The dog was big | The dog's head reached her waist, and its paws were the size of her fists |
| It was an old book | The book's spine cracked when opened, and the pages had yellowed to cream |

</details>

## âš–ï¸ **When to Tell Instead of Show**

**Not everything should be shown!** Strategic telling is essential for:

### **1. Pacing Control**

**TELL when you need to:**
- Move quickly through time
- Summarize unimportant events
- Transition between scenes
- Provide necessary context

**Example:**
> "Three months passed before she heard from him again."

This tells efficiently. Showing three months would bore readers.

---

### **2. Information Dumps (Minimally)**

**TELL when:**
- Backstory is essential but not dramatic
- Technical information is needed
- World-building requires explanation

**Example:**
> "The war had ended five years ago, but the city still bore its scars."

This tells efficiently, then you can show the scars.

---

### **3. Character Interiority**

**Internal thoughts** can tell directly when:
- You need the character's interpretation
- The feeling is complex
- Showing would be unclear

**Example:**
> "She knew, without understanding how, that her mother had lied."

This introspection works as telling.

---

### **4. Transitions**

**TELL for:**
- Time jumps
- Location changes
- Scene breaks

**Example:**
> "By the time they reached Denver, the snow had started."

## ğŸ¨ **Advanced Showing Techniques**

### **1. Implication**

Let readers infer rather than stating directly.

**TELL:**
> "He was poor."

**SHOW:**
> "He'd worn the same shirt three days running, and breakfast was the free samples at the grocery store."

**IMPLY:**
> "The apartment had furnitureâ€”a card table, a folding chair, a mattress on the floor. More than that seemed like tempting fate."

---

### **2. Contrast**

Show by contrasting with expectation.

**TELL:**
> "The party was awkward."

**SHOW WITH CONTRAST:**
> "The music was too loud, conversations too quiet. People laughed at the wrong moments and checked their phones like lifelines."

---

### **3. Metaphor and Simile**

Use comparison to show vividly.

**TELL:**
> "She was very thin."

**SHOW:**
> "Her wrists looked like they'd snap if you shook her hand too firmly."

---

### **4. Objective Correlative**

Show internal state through external objects/environment.

**TELL:**
> "He felt trapped in his life."

**SHOW (Objective Correlative):**
> "The office walls were the color of old teeth. The window didn't open. Every day, he wore the same tie."
