## ğŸ¨ **Types of Internal Monologue**

### **1. Direct Thought**

Character's exact thoughts, unfiltered and immediate.

**Formatted in italics (most common):**
> Sarah unlocked the door. *Please let him be home. Please.*

**Without italics (integrated):**
> Sarah unlocked the door. Please let him be home, she thought.

**Use for:**
- Intense emotional moments
- Urgent thoughts
- Character's exact words to themselves

---

### **2. Indirect Thought**

Reported thought, filtered through narration.

**Example:**
> Sarah wondered if he'd be home. She hoped so. She needed to talk to him.

**Use for:**
- Less intense moments
- Smoother narrative flow
- When voice is similar to narrative voice

---

### **3. Stream of Consciousness**

Unstructured, flowing thoughts mimicking how minds actually work.

**Example:**
> Keys. Did she have her keys? Pocketâ€”yes. And the report. Had to remember the report. God, she was tired. When was the last time she'd slept properly? Tuesday? Wednesday? The apartment would be dark. He'd be asleep already. She could be quiet. She was always quiet.

**Use for:**
- Heightened emotional states
- Showing scattered thinking
- Creating intimacy
- Literary effect

---

### **4. Free Indirect Discourse**

Blended narrator and character voiceâ€”thoughts woven seamlessly into narration.

**Example:**
> She opened the door slowly. Of course he wasn't home. Why would he be? He was never home anymore.

*No italics, no "she thought," but clearly her perspective and voice.*

**Use for:**
- Deep POV
- Smooth, invisible technique
- When narrator = character voice

## ğŸ¯ **When to Use Internal Monologue**

### **âœ… Use Internal Monologue When:**

**1. Processing Events**
> *He'd said he loved her. Past tense. When had that changed?*

**2. Making Decisions**
> *She could tell him. Right now. Get it over with. Or she could wait. Waiting was safer.*

**3. Revealing Motivation**
> *The money didn't matter. It had never been about the money. It was about proving he was wrong.*

> *Tell the truth. Lie to protect him. Tell the truth. Lie. Truth. Lie.*

> *The last time she'd been here, her mother was still alive.*

**6. Creating Dramatic Irony**
> *Everything would be fine. She was sure of it.*  
> *(Readers know: it won't be fine.)*

**7. Revealing Character Voice**
> *Great. Just great. Because this day couldn't possibly get any worse.*

## ğŸŒŠ **Varying Internal Monologue**

### **âœ… Best Practices:**

**1. Match Intensity to Moment**

**Low intensity:**
> She wondered if she'd locked the door.

**High intensity:**
> *The door. Had she locked it? She couldn't remember. God, she couldn't remember anything anymore.*

**2. Use Rhythm for Emotion**

**Calm, measured:**
> She had three options. She could call him. She could go to his house. Or she could do nothing.

**Panicked, fragmented:**
> Call him. No. Bad idea. Go to his house? Worse. Do nothing? Impossible.*

**3. Vary Sentence Length**

**Contemplative (long sentences):**
> *She'd known him for seventeen years, and in all that time, through everything they'd been through, she'd never once heard him admit he was wrong.*

**Urgent (short sentences):**
> *Run. Now. Don't look back.*

**4. Show Thought Patterns**

**Analytical character:**
> *The evidence pointed to three possibilities. First, he was lying. Second, he was mistaken. Thirdâ€”and this was the option she liked leastâ€”she was wrong.*

**Emotional character:**
> *He was lying. He had to be. Because if he wasn't, if this was real, if he actually meant it...*

**Cynical character:**
> *Of course he was lying. Everyone lied. That was the first rule: never trust anyone who said they cared.*

## ğŸ¨ **Deep POV: Maximizing Intimacy**

### **Deep POV = Minimal distance between reader and character**

**Standard POV:**

**Technique:**

- Weave thought into narration
- Use character's voice for description
- No separation between thought and narrative

## ğŸ”§ **Technical Considerations**

### **Formatting Direct Thought:**

**Option 1: Italics (most common)**
> *I can't believe he said that.*

**Option 2: Italics with thought tag**
> *I can't believe he said that,* she thought.

**Option 3: No italics, integrated**
> I can't believe he said that, she thought.

**Option 4: Free indirect (no markers)**
> She couldn't believe he'd said that.

**Consistency is key.** Choose one method and stick with it.

---

### **Thought Tags:**

**Common tags:**
- she thought
- he wondered
- she realized
- he remembered

**Use sparingly:**
- Context usually makes it clear it's thought
- Too many tags = clunky prose
- Deep POV often eliminates tags entirely

## ğŸ¯ **Common Internal Monologue Mistakes**

### **1. Too Much Introspection**

**Problem:**
> *Why did she feel this way? Was it because of her childhood? Her mother had always been distant. And her fatherâ€”well, he'd left when she was seven. That had shaped her, hadn't it? Made her afraid of abandonment. But was that really it? Or was there something else?*

**Solution:**
Introspection is good. Pages of it kills pacing. Break it up with action.

---

### **2. Telling Emotions in Thought**

**Problem:**
> *I'm sad. So sad. The sadness is overwhelming.*

**Solution:**
> *The apartment's too quiet. It's been three weeks and it's still too quiet.*

Show even in thought.

---

### **3. Unnatural Exposition**

**Problem:**
> *As I walked down the street I grew up on, I remembered that I was born here twenty-eight years ago, and my father was a lawyer, and I had two sisters...*

No one thinks like this.

**Solution:**
> The house looked smaller than she remembered. Twenty-eight years and it had shrunk.

Natural thought patterns.

---

### **4. Repetitive Thought Patterns**

**Problem:**
Every internal monologue starts with "I thought..." or uses the same structure.

**Solution:**
Vary your techniquesâ€”direct, indirect, free indirect. Mix it up.

---

### **5. Breaking Voice**

**Problem:**
> *Why do I persist in these endeavors when I know them to be fruitless?*

*(Character is a modern teenager.)*

**Solution:**
Thoughts should match how character actually thinks.

> *Why do I keep doing this? It's pointless.*

## ğŸ­ **Internal Monologue for Different POVs**

### **First Person:**
- Most natural for internal monologue
- Everything is thought, in a sense
- Narrator IS the character

**Example:**
> I couldn't look at him. If I looked at him, I'd cry, and I was done crying over Marcus.

---

### **Third Person Limited:**
- Internal monologue of POV character
- Can use direct or indirect thought
- Balance narration and thought

**Example:**
> She couldn't look at him. *If I look at him, I'll cry.* And she was done crying over Marcus.

---

### **Third Person Omniscient:**
- Can access multiple characters' thoughts
- Usually more distant
- Less intimate internal monologue

**Example:**
> She couldn't look at him. Marcus waited, hoping she would. Neither understood what the other needed.

---

### **Second Person:**
- "You" thinks the thoughts
- Intimate but unusual

**Example:**
> You can't look at him. If you look at him, you'll cry, and you're done crying over Marcus.
