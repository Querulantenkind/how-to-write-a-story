## ğŸ¬ **Types of Action**

### **1. Physical Action**

What the character **does** in the physical world.

**Examples:**
- "She slammed the door"
- "He counted to ten before answering"
- "They walked three feet apart"

### **2. Verbal Action**

What the character **says** (and how they say it).

- "She spoke slowly, choosing each word like stepping on ice."

**Use for:**
- Revealing character relationships
- Advancing plot through dialogue
- Showing internal state through speech

### **3. Emotional Action**

**Use for:**
- POV character's internal world
- Connecting readers emotionally
- Building tension

## ğŸ­ **Reaction Beats**

### **The Dwight Swain Model**

Reader-friendly reactions follow this natural order:

1. **Feeling** (visceral, immediate)
2. **Reflex** (instinctive physical response)
3. **Rational Thought** (processing)
4. **Speech or Action** (deliberate response)

**Example:**

**Stimulus:** A gunshot rings out.

1. **Feeling:** "Fear spiked through her"
2. **Reflex:** "She dropped to the ground"
3. **Thought:** *Where did it come from?*
4. **Action:** "She crawled toward the doorway"

### **Not Every Beat Every Time:**

- Fast scenes skip to reflex/action
- Slow scenes linger in thought
- Vary for pacing

## âš–ï¸ **Balancing Action & Reaction**

### **âœ… Best Practices:**

**1. Match Intensity:**
- Small stimulus = small reaction
- Major event = strong reaction
- Don't over-react to trivial things

**2. Stay in Character:**
- Reactions should fit personality
- Build character through consistency
- Vary reactions with character growth

**3. Use Action for Pacing:**
- Fast pacing = quick, decisive actions
- Slow pacing = contemplative reactions
- Control rhythm through behavior

**4. Avoid Repetition:**
- Characters shouldn't always react the same way
- Vary physical tics and patterns
- Find fresh ways to show emotions

**5. Make It Specific:**
- Generic: "She was upset"
- Specific: "She tore the letter into perfect squares"

## âŒ **Common Mistakes**

### **1. Over-Explaining:**

**Too Much:**
> "He slammed the door because he was angry that she'd lied to him, which made him feel betrayed and furious."

**Better:**
> "He slammed the door."

*The action is enough. Trust your readers.*

### **2. Unearned Reactions:**

**Unearned:**
> "She'd known him for five minutes when he told her his name, and suddenly she loved him more than life itself."

**Earned:**
> "She'd known him for three years. She knew how he took his coffee, the scar on his left hand, the way he hummed when he was nervous. And somewhere in those thousand small moments, she'd fallen in love."

### **3. Telling After Showing:**

**Redundant:**
> "He clenched his fists, his face red, jaw tight. He was angry."

**Better:**
> "He clenched his fists, his face red, jaw tight."

*You already showed it. Don't tell it too.*

### **4. Every Character Reacts the Same:**

**Problem:**
> Everyone cries when sad, everyone yells when angry, everyone's knees buckle when scared.

**Solution:**
Diversify reactions based on personality, background, and situation.

## ğŸ¯ **Practice Exercises**

### **Exercise 1: Reaction Variety**

Write 10 different ways a character could react to receiving bad news. No two reactions should be the same.

### **Exercise 2: Character Through Action**

Write three characters responding to the same situation (e.g., finding a wallet full of cash). Use only actionâ€”no internal monologue or telling. The differences in their actions should reveal their personalities.

### **Exercise 3: The Reaction Beat**

Take a pivotal moment in your story. Write the character's reaction using Dwight Swain's four-beat pattern:
1. Feeling
2. Reflex
3. Thought
4. Action

### **Exercise 4: Subtext Through Action**

Write a scene where a character says one thing but their actions show they mean the opposite. The reader should understand the truth without you stating it.

### **Exercise 5: Escalation**

Write a character's reaction to the same bad news three times:
- **Version 1:** Understated, subtle
- **Version 2:** Moderate, balanced
- **Version 3:** Extreme, over-the-top

Identify which version fits your story best.
