### **Personal Stories and Experiences**

**When Someone Shares Their Story:**

**Consider:**
- Did they give permission for you to use it?
- Could they be identified, even indirectly?
- Would publication cause them harm or embarrassment?
- Is their story yours to tell?

**Best Practice:**
> Get **explicit permission** before using someone's personal experience, even if changed/fictionalized.

**Example:**
> "A friend told me about her experience with X. Can I use that as inspiration for a character, with details changed?" 

If they say yes: Get it in writing (even just an email).
If they say no: Respect it completely.
If you're unsure: Ask clearly.

## ğŸŒ **Cultural Sensitivity and Representation**

### **Writing Outside Your Culture**

**First Question: Should I?**

Before writing characters from cultures/identities different from your own, honestly ask:
- Why am I telling this story?
- Am I the right person to tell it?
- Could someone from that culture tell it better?
- Am I taking space from marginalized voices?
- What are my motivations?

**Not automatically a "no"** â€” but requires **deep thought and extra care**.

---

### **If You Proceed:**

**Required Steps:**

âœ… **Deep Research**
- Not just Wikipedia
- Multiple sources
- Primary sources when possible
- Contemporary voices from that culture
- Historical and modern perspectives

âœ… **Sensitivity Readers**
- **Essential**, not optional
- Hire readers from the culture/identity you're writing
- Pay them fairly (going rate: $0.01-0.03/word or $250+ per manuscript)
- Listen to their feedback
- Be willing to make changes

âœ… **Avoid Stereotypes**
- Research common stereotypesâ€”then avoid them
- Show diversity within the culture
- Avoid making culture the character's only trait
- Show individual complexity

âœ… **Understand Nuance**
- Cultures aren't monolithic
- Individual experience varies
- Historical vs. contemporary differences
- Regional variations

âœ… **Consider Power Dynamics**
- Who has historically told stories about this culture?
- What harm have inaccurate portrayals caused?
- How can you avoid perpetuating harm?

### **Mental Illness and Disability**

**Ethical Considerations:**

**Avoid:**
- âŒ Mental illness as villain motivation only
- âŒ Disability existing only to inspire others ("inspiration porn")
- âŒ Magical cure narratives
- âŒ Outdated/offensive terminology
- âŒ Conflating different conditions

**Do:**
- âœ… Research the actual experience
- âœ… Show diverse experiences (not everyone with X condition experiences it the same)
- âœ… Include disability without making it the entire character
- âœ… Consult sensitivity readers with lived experience
- âœ… Portray accommodation and adaptation
- âœ… Recognize disabled people as full humans

**Resources:**

- Medical professionals AND people with lived experience

---

### **Trauma and Violence**

**Ethical Considerations:**

**Sexual Violence:**
- Is it necessary to the story?
- Are you handling it with appropriate gravity?
- Are you avoiding gratuitous detail?
- Does it serve the story or just shock value?
- Are you considering survivor readers?

**Child Abuse:**
- Extra care required
- Consider: does it need to be on-page?
- Focus on aftermath and resilience, not graphic details
- Consult trauma experts if central to plot

**Hate Crimes and Historical Atrocities:**
- Research thoroughly and respectfully
- Avoid sensationalizing
- Consider descendants of victims
- Show dignity even in depicting horror
- Understand historical context fully

**General Guidelines:**
- Focus on impact rather than graphic detail
- Consider reader triggers (content warnings)
- Avoid using trauma as character development shortcut
- Show realistic aftermath and recovery (or lack thereof)
- Consult trauma survivors and experts

---

### **Suicide and Self-Harm**

**Critical Considerations:**

**Research Guidelines:**
- Avoid detailed descriptions of methods
- Don't romanticize or glorify
- Show realistic consequences
- Include resources in author's note if topic is central
- Consider contagion effect

**Responsible Portrayal:**
- Mental illness is complex (not just sadness)
- Suicide is not romantic or noble
- Show impact on others
- Avoid "successful suicide solves problems" narratives
- If showing recovery, make it realistic

## ğŸ‘¥ **Real People in Fiction**

### **Public Figures**

**Legal Issues:**
- Defamation laws vary by country
- Public figures have less protection than private citizens
- Truth is a defense, but can still cause legal issues

**Ethical Issues:**
- Even if legal, is it right?
- Would this harm their reputation unfairly?
- Are you perpetuating false narratives?
- What about their family?

**Best Practice:**
- Fictionalize significantly
- Use composites rather than individuals
- Change names and identifying details
- Consider: is using real person necessary?

---

### **Historical Figures**

**More Freedom, but Still:**
- Research thoroughly
- Represent fairly (even villains had complexity)
- Acknowledge gaps in historical record
- Don't impose modern values unfairly
- Consider descendants

---

### **Private Individuals**

**Essential:**
- Get **written permission**
- Change identifying details significantly
- Consider impact on their life
- Respect their "no" completely
- Never expose private information without consent

## ğŸ“š **Academic and Professional Ethics**

### **Using Others' Research**

**Proper Attribution:**
- Don't present others' theories as your own
- Acknowledge sources in author's note
- If heavily based on one source, credit it prominently
- Respect copyright on quoted material

**Fair Use (Generally):**
- Brief quotes for commentary/criticism
- Transformative use
- Not substituting for original
- Varies by countryâ€”check local laws

**When in Doubt:**
- Credit your sources
- Ask permission for substantial quotes
- Hire lawyers for complex situations

---

### **Plagiarism in Research**

**Even in Fiction:**
- Don't copy non-fiction sources directly into prose
- Paraphrase and transform
- Ideas can be used; exact language should not be stolen
- Credit unusual or unique theories/ideas
