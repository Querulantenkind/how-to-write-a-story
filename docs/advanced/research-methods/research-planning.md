# üìÖ Research Planning

> *Strategic approaches to research‚Äîknowing when, what, and how much*

---

## üìñ **Overview**

Good research planning prevents two common pitfalls:
1. **Research paralysis:** Getting stuck researching forever
2. **Research gaps:** Writing yourself into corners with missing knowledge

The key is finding the balance between **enough knowledge to write confidently** and **enough momentum to actually write**.

---

## ‚è∞ **When to Research**

### **Before Writing (Upfront Research)**

**Best For:**
- Historical settings (need time period basics)
- Technical/specialized fields (medical, legal, military)
- Unfamiliar locations (major geography and culture)
- Plot-critical details (the story hinges on accuracy)

**What to Research:**
- ‚úÖ Core facts that affect plot structure
- ‚úÖ Time period basics and major events
- ‚úÖ Setting geography and climate
- ‚úÖ Major cultural or social norms
- ‚úÖ Technical processes central to plot
- ‚úÖ Character profession fundamentals

**What to Skip:**
- ‚ùå Every tiny detail
- ‚ùå Tangential background information
- ‚ùå Atmospheric details (get these during writing)
- ‚ùå Minor character specializations
- ‚ùå Specific dialogue phrasing

**Time Limit:**
Set a deadline. Example: "Two weeks of research, then I start drafting."

---

### **During Writing (Just-in-Time Research)**

**Best For:**
- Discovery writers (pantsers)
- Maintaining writing momentum
- Unexpected plot developments
- Character voice development

**Method:**

1. **Mark as you go:**
   ```
   She walked into the [RESEARCH: typical 1950s diner layout] 
   and ordered [RESEARCH: popular menu items era].
   ```

2. **Keep writing** past the gap

3. **Quick check** only if it affects immediate plot
   - Use 5-minute rule: If you can't find it in 5 minutes, mark it and move on

4. **Research in batches** during natural breaks
   - End of writing session
   - Between chapters
   - During planned rest days

**Advantages:**
- ‚úÖ Maintains momentum
- ‚úÖ Research only what you actually need
- ‚úÖ Stays focused on story
- ‚úÖ Less procrastination temptation

---

### **Between Drafts (Batch Research)**

**Best For:**
- Filling in marked gaps
- Deepening existing scenes
- Fact-checking completed draft
- Adding authentic details

**Process:**

1. **Compile** all [RESEARCH] tags from draft
2. **Categorize** by topic/type
3. **Prioritize** (plot-critical first)
4. **Batch** similar research tasks
5. **Set time limits** for each category
6. **Apply** findings to revision

**Example Research List:**
```
HIGH PRIORITY:
- [ ] How does a submarine emergency surface work? (Ch. 15)
- [ ] Can DNA tests be rushed? How long? (Ch. 22)
- [ ] Victorian mourning customs for widow (Ch. 3, 8, 19)

MEDIUM PRIORITY:
- [ ] Typical 1920s women's day wear (Ch. 2)
- [ ] Paris Metro stations near Montmartre (Ch. 12)
- [ ] What could doctor diagnose without X-ray? (Ch. 7)

LOW PRIORITY:
- [ ] French breakfast foods (Ch. 1)
- [ ] Common first names 1890s England (various)
- [ ] Garden flowers in July, London (Ch. 9)
```

---

### **During Revision (Verification Research)**

**Best For:**
- Fact-checking specific claims
- Catching anachronisms
- Verifying technical accuracy
- Adding sensory details

**What to Check:**
- ‚úÖ Dates and timelines
- ‚úÖ Technical processes
- ‚úÖ Historical facts
- ‚úÖ Geography and travel times
- ‚úÖ Professional procedures
- ‚úÖ Period-appropriate technology
- ‚úÖ Cultural details

## üéØ **Determining Research Scope**

### **Core Questions:**

**1. What Genre Are You Writing?**
- **Historical fiction:** Heavy research required
- **Contemporary realistic:** Moderate research
- **Fantasy/SF:** World-building research (different type)
- **Thriller/Mystery:** Specialized technical research

**2. What's Your Story's Relationship to Reality?**
- **Tightly realistic:** High accuracy needed
- **Heightened reality:** Some creative license
- **Loosely inspired:** Broad authenticity sufficient

**3. Who's Your Target Audience?**
- **Expert readers:** They'll catch errors‚Äîresearch thoroughly
- **General audience:** Need plausibility, not perfection
- **Young readers:** Age-appropriate complexity

**4. What's Plot-Critical vs. Background?**
- **Plot-critical:** MUST be accurate (affects plot logic)
- **Character-defining:** Should be accurate (affects credibility)
- **Atmospheric:** Can be approximate (creates mood)

## üìä **The Research Depth Matrix**

| Element | Minimal Research | Moderate Research | Deep Research |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Time investment** | Hours | Days | Weeks |
| **Source types** | Wikipedia, Google | Books, articles, videos | Archives, experts, primary sources |
| **Detail level** | General plausibility | Specific accuracy | Expert-level knowledge |
| **Use case** | Background elements | Supporting details | Core plot elements |

### **Example Applications:**

**Minimal:** Your protagonist walks past a construction site (not relevant to plot)
- Quick Google: What equipment is used? What sounds?
- Good enough: "Jackhammers, crane, workers in hard hats"

**Moderate:** Your protagonist's sister is a pediatric nurse
- Read articles about pediatric nursing
- Watch YouTube day-in-the-life videos
- Note key details for authentic references

**Deep:** Your protagonist IS a pediatric nurse solving a medical mystery
- Interview actual pediatric nurses
- Read nursing textbooks
- Shadow at a hospital if possible
- Join nursing forums
- Understand terminology, procedures, culture

## üó∫Ô∏è **Creating a Research Plan**

### **Step 1: Identify Knowledge Gaps**

**Brainstorm:**
- What do I need to know to write this story?
- What could readers with expertise catch me on?
- What feels fuzzy or uncertain in my outline?
- What would my character need to know?

**Categorize Gaps:**
```
MUST KNOW (Can't write without it):
- Time period: Social norms, technology, daily life
- Setting: Geography, climate, culture
- Plot device: How the core mechanism works

SHOULD KNOW (Affects credibility):
- Character profession: Terminology, daily routines
- Specialized skills: Accurate process details
- Cultural context: Customs, beliefs, conflicts

NICE TO KNOW (Enriches story):
- Period details: Fashion, food, entertainment
- Local flavor: Dialect, landmarks, traditions
- Background texture: Historical events, famous figures
```

---

### **Step 2: Set Research Parameters**

**Time Budget:**
- How much time before you MUST start writing?
- Daily/weekly research time allocation
- Hard deadline for beginning draft

**Financial Budget:**
- Book purchases or library access
- Travel to locations
- Expert consultation fees
- Course or workshop fees

**Priority Ranking:**
1. What's needed to start writing Chapter 1?
2. What's needed for main plot development?
3. What can wait until revision?
4. What's optional enrichment?

---

### **Step 3: Create Research Schedule**

**Week 1-2: Foundational Research**
- Historical context
- Setting basics
- Main character profession
- Core plot mechanism

**Week 3: Specialized Details**
- Technical processes
- Supporting character backgrounds
- Secondary locations

**Week 4: Cultural/Atmospheric**
- Period flavor
- Sensory details
- Cultural context

**Then: START WRITING**

---

### **Example: Historical Mystery Research Plan**

**Story:** Murder mystery set in 1889 London, protagonist is female journalist

**Phase 1 (Week 1): Foundational‚ÄîBEFORE WRITING**
- [ ] London geography 1889
- [ ] Women journalists: Were they allowed? How?
- [ ] Police investigation procedures 1889
- [ ] Daily life: Transportation, communication, money
- [ ] Major historical events of the year

**Phase 2 (Weeks 2-3): During First Draft**
- [ ] Specific neighborhoods and landmarks
- [ ] Women's clothing (practical research‚Äîhow restrictive?)
- [ ] Newspaper operations and printing
- [ ] Medical knowledge and autopsy procedures
- [ ] Social class differences and interactions

**Phase 3 (Between Drafts): Batch Research**
- [ ] All [RESEARCH] tags from draft
- [ ] Victorian slang and speech patterns
- [ ] Food, drink, entertainments
- [ ] Crime statistics and famous cases
- [ ] Working conditions for journalists

**Phase 4 (Revision): Verification**
- [ ] Fact-check all specific dates
- [ ] Verify all London locations still existed
- [ ] Check technology (telephone availability?)
- [ ] Confirm all social details
- [ ] Catch anachronisms

## üö´ **Avoiding Research Rabbit Holes**

### **Warning Signs You're in a Rabbit Hole:**

- ‚ö†Ô∏è You've been researching the same topic for days
- ‚ö†Ô∏è You're researching details completely tangential to your story
- ‚ö†Ô∏è You're reading your 47th article "just to be sure"
- ‚ö†Ô∏è You're afraid to start writing until you know "everything"
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Research feels like procrastination (be honest!)
- ‚ö†Ô∏è You've forgotten what you originally needed to know

### **Escape Strategies:**

**1. Set Timer**
"I'll research this topic for 1 hour, then stop whether I'm done or not."

**2. Question Test**
Write down your specific question. When you can answer it, stop.

**3. Good Enough Threshold**
"Do I know enough to write this scene? Yes? Then stop researching."

**4. Source Limit**
"I'll consult 3 sources max, then move on."

**5. Accountability**
Tell someone your research deadline and writing start date.

**6. Remember Your Goal**
"I'm writing a novel, not a dissertation."

## ‚è±Ô∏è **Research Time Management**

### **The Research Time Budget**

**For 300-page novel:**

**Historical or technical:** 40-80 hours research
- 20-40 hours upfront
- 10-20 hours during drafting
- 10-20 hours in revision

**Contemporary realistic:** 15-30 hours research
- 5-10 hours upfront
- 5-10 hours during drafting
- 5-10 hours verification

**Fantasy/Science Fiction:** Variable
- World-building research (science, history, culture)
- Less "fact-checking" but more internal consistency

**Evening:** Light research
- Browsing reference materials
- Watching documentaries

## üí° **Pro Tips**

### **1. The 80/20 Rule**
80% of research value comes from 20% of your research time. Focus on high-impact essentials first.

### **2. Research in Layers**
- Layer 1: Broad overview (Wikipedia, documentaries)
- Layer 2: Specific details (books, articles)
- Layer 3: Expert depth (interviews, primary sources)

Not every topic needs Layer 3.

### **3. Keep a "Questions" Document**
When questions arise during writing, jot them down. Research in batches during breaks.

### **4. Schedule Research Sessions**
Treat research like writing appointments. "Tuesday, 9-11am: Victorian clothing research."

### **5. Know When to Use Placeholders**
Can't find the perfect period-appropriate greeting? Write "[GREETING]" and move on.
