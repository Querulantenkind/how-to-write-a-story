## üìñ **What is Realism?**

**Realism** (roughly 1850-1900) was a revolutionary literary movement that rejected Romanticism's idealization in favor of **objective, detailed representation of everyday life**‚Äîparticularly middle and lower-class experience.

**Core Principle:** Depict life as it actually is, not as we wish it to be.

## üéØ **Historical Context**

### **Why Realism Emerged:**

**Social Changes:**
- Industrial Revolution transforming society
- Rise of middle class
- Urban growth and social problems
- Scientific thinking and empiricism
- Photography demonstrating "objective" reality

**Intellectual Climate:**
- Positivism ‚Äî Truth through observation
- Darwin's evolution ‚Äî Natural world without romance

**Reaction Against:**

- Heroic, extraordinary subjects

## ‚ú® **Core Principles**

### **1. Objective Observation**

Present reality without authorial judgment or romanticism.

**In Practice:**
- Detailed, accurate description
- Multiple perspectives
- Avoid editorial commentary
- Show, don't tell (before it was trendy)
- Reporter's eye for detail

**Example Approach:**
```
Romantic: "The noble peasant, his soul uncorrupted 
by civilization's vices, toiled with dignity."

Realist: "The farmhand's hands were cracked and bleeding. 
He coughed‚Äîthe same cough he'd had for three months. 
Tomorrow would be the same as today: wake, work, sleep, 
with barely enough food to sustain him."
```

---

### **2. Ordinary People, Ordinary Lives**

Focus on middle and working class, not aristocrats and heroes.

**Subjects:**
- Merchants and shopkeepers
- Farmers and laborers
- Servants and factory workers
- Small-town life
- Family struggles
- Economic pressures

**Why This Mattered:**
Previously, literature focused on:
- Nobility and upper classes
- Extraordinary events
- Historical or exotic settings
- Exceptional individuals

Realism said: **Ordinary lives are worthy subjects.**

---

### **3. Social Context Matters**

Characters shaped by social, economic, and historical forces.

**Environmental Determinism:**
- Class determines opportunities
- Social forces limit choices
- Historical moment shapes character
- Economic reality constrains action

**In Practice:**
- Characters' social class detailed
- Economic circumstances drive plot
- Historical setting precisely rendered
- Social institutions examined (marriage, church, government)

---

### **4. Plausible Plots**

No miraculous coincidences or melodramatic contrivances.

**Realistic Plot:**
- Events that could actually happen
- Cause-and-effect logic
- Problems without easy solutions
- Endings that follow logically, not neatly
- Consequences matter

**Rejected:**
- Sudden inheritances solving problems
- Love conquering all obstacles
- Villains conveniently dying
- Perfect happy endings
- Supernatural intervention

---

### **5. Accurate Psychology**

Complex, believable characters with mixed motives.

**Psychological Realism:**
- Characters have flaws and virtues
- Motivations are complex
- Internal contradictions
- Gradual character development
- No pure heroes or villains

## üÜö **Realism vs. Romanticism**

| Aspect | Romanticism | Realism |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Subject** | Exceptional individuals | Ordinary people |
| **Setting** | Exotic, historical, natural | Contemporary, familiar |
| **Plot** | Dramatic, improbable | Plausible, everyday |
| **Emotion** | Intense, passionate | Restrained, complex |
| **Style** | Elevated, poetic | Clear, precise |
| **Values** | Emotion, imagination | Observation, truth |
| **Character** | Idealized or demonized | Mixed, complex |
| **Ending** | Triumphant or tragic | Ambiguous, realistic |

## ‚úçÔ∏è **Realist Techniques**

### **Descriptive Detail**

**The Realist Eye:**
- Precise, not poetic
- Accumulation of specific details
- Creates sense of reality through specificity
- Everything has a purpose

**Example:**
```
"The drawing room contained a horsehair sofa 
(recovering from its third upholstering), a 
mahogany table with one leg slightly shorter 
than the others (compensated by a folded paper), 
three chairs in different styles (acquired over 
years as budget allowed), and a clock that ran 
seven minutes fast‚Äîa fact the family had long 
since stopped noticing."
```

---

### **Free Indirect Discourse**

Blend of narrator and character perspective.

**How It Works:**
- Third person narration
- Adopts character's vocabulary/viewpoint
- No quotation marks
- Maintains narrative distance while showing interiority

**Example:**
```
Direct thought: She thought, "What will the neighbors say?"
Free indirect: What would the neighbors say? The question 
tormented her through the sleepless night.
```

**Why Realists Loved It:**
- Maintains objectivity
- Shows character psychology
- Allows irony
- No romantic "I" confessions

---

### **Showing Character Through Action**

Rather than telling us someone is greedy, show them act greedily.

**Method:**
- Dialogue reveals character
- Actions demonstrate values
- Small choices show personality
- Habitual behaviors define

**Example:**
```
Don't tell: "John was stingy."
Show: "John calculated the tip to exactly fifteen percent, 
rounding down to the nearest nickel."
```

---

### **Social Milieu**

Create complete social world.

**Elements:**
- Economic details (what things cost, how people earn)
- Social hierarchies and interactions
- Historical/political context
- Physical environment reflecting social reality
- Institutions (church, government, business)

## üé® **Realist Themes**

### **Common Concerns:**

**1. Social Class**
- Class barriers
- Social mobility (or lack thereof)
- Economic inequality
- Manners and mores of different classes

**2. Marriage and Women**
- Marriage as economic arrangement
- Women's limited options
- Consequences of divorce/separation
- Female education and employment

**3. Urban vs. Rural**
- Industrialization's impact
- City life contrasted with country
- Migration and dislocation
- Traditional ways disappearing

**4. Money and Work**
- Economic pressure
- Debt and poverty
- Professional life
- Inheritance and property

**5. Moral Complexity**
- No easy answers
- Good people do bad things
- Bad people have reasons
- Context matters

## üåä **Naturalism: Extreme Realism**

**Naturalism** (1870s-1940s) took realism further.

**Key Differences:**
- **Determinism** ‚Äî People controlled by heredity, environment, chance
- **Darker vision** ‚Äî Life as struggle for survival
- **Scientific approach** ‚Äî Humans as specimens to study
- **Pessimistic** ‚Äî Often bleak outcomes
- **Lower classes** ‚Äî Focus on poorest, most desperate

**Major Naturalists:**
- **√âmile Zola** ‚Äî Experimental novel
- **Stephen Crane** ‚Äî *Maggie: A Girl of the Streets*
- **Frank Norris** ‚Äî *McTeague*
- **Theodore Dreiser** ‚Äî *Sister Carrie*
- **Jack London** ‚Äî Survival stories

**In Practice:**
```
Realist: "Poverty limited his choices, but he tried his best."
Naturalist: "Born to poverty, damaged by alcoholic father, 
he never had a chance. His fate was sealed before he could walk."
```

## üìù **Writing Exercise**

**Realist Character Study:**

Create a character from a specific social class and time period. Write a scene showing (not telling):
- Their economic situation
- Social pressures they face
- A difficult choice with no perfect solution
- Their complex, mixed motivations

Use specific details and free indirect discourse. No melodrama.
